Day 01 · Grammar: the nominal sentence

The Sentence
Without a Verb

Before you can understand a lecture, you must learn to hear its skeleton. Arabic hangs everything on a very small number of frames. Today you learn the first one.

Section 1Objectives

  1. Recognise 15 of the highest-frequency words in Modern Standard Arabic — words you will hear in almost every lecture, interview and documentary you ever watch.
  2. Understand and build the nominal sentence (الجُمْلَةُ الاسْمِيَّةُ) — an Arabic sentence with no verb at all.
  3. Hear the difference between "the important matter" and "the matter is important" — a distinction Arabic marks with no extra word.
  4. Follow a short spoken passage built entirely from today's vocabulary.
Why we start here

Roughly a third of the sentences in a typical Arabic lecture contain no verb at all. The speaker says "This is the point," "The matter is clear," "Knowledge is light." Lock onto this frame today and you have bought yourself a permanent listening advantage.

Section 2Vocabulary — 15 Core Words

Study tip

Do not try to memorise these. Read each one aloud three times, read its example sentence once, and move on. You will meet all fifteen again in Chapters 2, 3 and 5. Repetition across days beats effort in one sitting.

01قَالَ
he said
verb · past ق – و – ل Top 20 spoken
قَالَ الرَّجُلُ: هٰذَا حَقٌّ.

The man said: this is true.

Hear it in: قَالَ اللهُ تَعَالَىٰ · كَمَا قَالَ (as he said)

02ذَكَرَ
he mentioned; he recalled
verb · past ذ – ك – ر The "footnote verb"
ذَكَرَ الْعِلْمَ فِي كَلَامِهِ.

He mentioned knowledge in his speech.

Root family

From this same root come ذِكْر (remembrance) and تَذْكِير (reminding). Roots are Arabic's superpower: learn one, get a family free.

03كَانَ
he / it was
verb · past ك – و – ن Top 10
كَانَ هٰذَا أَمْرًا مُهِمًّا.

This was an important matter.

Arabic has no present-tense "is" — but it certainly has a past-tense "was". That is كَانَ. (Ignore the -an endings for now; Chapter 4 unlocks them.)

04هٰذَا
this (masculine)
demonstrative Top 15

Section 3Ten Common Sentences

These are not textbook sentences. These are sentences you will hear this week if you open any Arabic lecture on YouTube.

01
هٰذَا أَمْرٌ مُهِمٌّ جِدًّا.

This is a very important matter.

When you hear it: the speaker is flagging what comes next. Treat it as an audio highlighter.

02
قَالَ اللهُ تَعَالَىٰ...

Allah, exalted is He, said...

When you hear it: the single most common phrase in Islamic lectures. A Qur'anic verse always follows.

03
كَمَا ذَكَرْنَا مِنْ قَبْلُ.

As we mentioned before.

When you hear it: a callback to an earlier point.

04
هٰذَا هُوَ الْحَقُّ.

This is the truth.

When you hear it: a conclusion, usually emphatic. Note the little هُوَ in the middle — Section 4 explains exactly why it is there.

05
عِنْدِي سُؤَالٌ.

I have a question.

When you hear it: Q&A sessions, interviews, classrooms.

06
النَّاسُ فِي هٰذَا الزَّمَانِ...

People in this age...

When you hear it: the opening of a social or moral commentary. Almost a genre signal.

07
هَلْ عِنْدَكَ شَيْءٌ آخَرُ؟

Do you have anything else?

When you hear it: conversation, interviews, the end of a discussion point.

08
الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ.

Knowledge is light.

When you hear it: proverbial — and your first perfect two-word verbless sentence.

09
هُنَاكَ أَشْيَاءُ كَثِيرَةٌ.

There are many things.

When you hear it: right before a list. Get ready to count on your fingers.

10
تَكَلَّمْنَا عَنْ هٰذَا مِنْ قَبْلُ.

We spoke about this before.

When you hear it: the speaker is skipping ahead, assuming you were listening last time.

Section 4Grammar — The Nominal Sentence

الجُمْلَةُ الاسْمِيَّةُ

The rule in one line

Arabic has no word for "is." Put two nouns next to each other, and Arabic hears a complete sentence.

الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ.

Knowledge is light.

Two words. Full sentence. The "is" lives in the silence between them. The structure has a name and two parts:

PartArabic nameRoleHere
Subjectالْمُبْتَدَأ al-mubtada'what we are talking aboutالْعِلْمُ
Predicateالْخَبَر al-khabarwhat we say about itنُورٌ
The trap that catches everyone

These two differ by one syllable — and they are completely different animals.

الأَمْرُ الْمُهِمُّ

the important matter — a phrase, not a sentence. Both words carry الـ.

الأَمْرُ مُهِمٌّ.

The matter is important — a full sentence. The first word has الـ; the second does not.

StructureResult
definite + definitea phrase — noun + adjective
definite + indefinitea sentence — "X is Y"
Listening tip

Train your ear for the final -un — the tanwīn ٌ. When you hear الْأَمْرُ مُهِمٌّ, that -un is your signal: this is a claim, not a label. This one habit will visibly speed up your comprehension within a week.

And what about هُوَ in the middle?

Remember sentence 4: هٰذَا هُوَ الْحَقُّ. Why the extra "he/it"? Because both words are definite, so Arabic would otherwise read them as a phrase — "this truth." The pronoun هُوَ is dropped in as a separator, the ضَمِير الْفَصْل ("pronoun of separation"). It carries no meaning of its own. It simply announces: these two are subject and predicate, not noun and adjective.

You will hear it constantly. Now it will never confuse you.

Negating a nominal sentence

Arabic has a dedicated word for "is not": لَيْسَ.

لَيْسَ الْأَمْرُ سَهْلًا.

The matter is not easy.

We study لَيْسَ properly in Chapter 6. For now, just recognise it: لَيْسَ = "is not."

Section 5Reading Practice

الْعِلْمُ وَالنَّاسُ

الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ. هٰذَا أَمْرٌ مُهِمٌّ جِدًّا. النَّاسُ كَثِيرُونَ، وَالْعِلْمُ عِنْدَ النَّاسِ لَيْسَ وَاحِدًا.

هٰذَا هُوَ الرَّجُلُ الَّذِي ذَكَرَ الْحَقَّ. قَالَ: كُلُّ شَيْءٍ فِي الدُّنْيَا كَثِيرٌ، وَالْعِلْمُ قَلِيلٌ.

هٰذِهِ مَسْأَلَةٌ مُهِمَّةٌ، وَنَحْنُ نَتَكَلَّمُ عَنْهَا كَثِيرًا.

Knowledge and People

Knowledge is light. This is a very important matter. People are many, and knowledge among people is not one and the same.

This is the man who mentioned the truth. He said: everything in this world is plentiful, but knowledge is scarce.

This is an important issue, and we speak about it often.

New in context: وَاحِد one · الدُّنْيَا this world · قَلِيل few, scarce · نَحْنُ we · نَتَكَلَّمُ we speak

Notice

Four sentences in that passage have no verb: الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ · هٰذَا أَمْرٌ مُهِمٌّ · الْعِلْمُ قَلِيلٌ · هٰذِهِ مَسْأَلَةٌ مُهِمَّةٌ. That is not a coincidence — it is how Arabic thinks.

Section 6Listening Practice

Read this aloud twice — once slowly, once at natural speed. Better still, record yourself and listen back. Your ear learns from your own mouth.

Scenario: the opening thirty seconds of a lecture.

الْمُحَاضِرُ:

بِسْمِ اللهِ. الْيَوْمَ نَتَكَلَّمُ عَنْ أَمْرٍ مُهِمٍّ جِدًّا.

كَمَا ذَكَرْنَا مِنْ قَبْلُ، الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ. وَهٰذَا هُوَ الْحَقُّ.

النَّاسُ فِي هٰذَا الزَّمَانِ عِنْدَهُمْ أَشْيَاءُ كَثِيرَةٌ، وَلٰكِنْ... هَلْ عِنْدَهُمُ الْعِلْمُ؟

هٰذِهِ هِيَ الْمَسْأَلَةُ.

طَالِبٌ:

عِنْدِي سُؤَالٌ، يَا أُسْتَاذُ.

الْمُحَاضِرُ:

نَعَمْ، تَفَضَّلْ.

Lecturer: In the name of Allah. Today we are speaking about a very important matter. As we mentioned before, knowledge is light. And this is the truth. People in this age have many things — but... do they have knowledge? That is the question.

Student: I have a question, teacher.

Lecturer: Yes, go ahead.

New: الْمُحَاضِر lecturer · الْيَوْم today · وَلٰكِنْ but · عِنْدَهُمْ they have · طَالِب student · تَفَضَّلْ go ahead

Did you catch it?

هٰذِهِ هِيَ الْمَسْأَلَةُ — there is the pronoun of separation again, this time feminine. You learned the rule ten minutes ago and you have already used it to decode real speech. That is what this whole book is going to feel like.

Section 7Word Sheets

Every verb in Arabic lives in four rooms: what was done (الماضي), what is being done (المضارع), what you are told to do (الأمر), and what you are told not to do (النهي). Learn a verb in one room only and you will not recognise it in the other three.

Each grid is the full تَصْرِيف — all fourteen persons, duals and feminine plurals included. That is more than you need for speech, but exactly what you need for listening: the Qur'an and the classical books use every one of these forms, and an unfamiliar tail is the fastest way to lose a sentence. The first six rows — shaded — carry the overwhelming bulk of real speech. They come first for a reason: read those aloud every time. The duals and feminine plurals below them are there because the Qur'an and the classical books use them, and an unfamiliar tail is the fastest way to lose a sentence — but let your eye simply pass over them until they start to look familiar on their own.

Read the هُوَ and أَنَا rows aloud for every verb — those two are what you will hear and say most. Then glance at the command form and ask: have I heard this in the Qur'an? Very often, you have.

Section 8Derivation Table

The grids above answered one question: who did it? This table answers a different one, and a bigger one — what else can this root become?

Arabic does not really have words. It has roots, and a set of moulds you pour them into. Pour ك ت ب into the doer-mould and you get كَاتِب, a writer. Pour it into the done-to mould and you get مَكْتُوب, something written. The moulds never change. Learn them once and every root in the language starts handing you free words.

The two moulds that matter most

The doer — فَاعِل: fāʿil. Take a root, pour it in: قَائِل (a speaker), ذَاكِر (one who remembers), كَاتِب (a writer).

The done-to — مَفْعُول: mafʿūl. Same root, other mould: مَقُول (what is said), مَذْكُور (mentioned), مَكْتُوب (written, decreed).

And notice — fāʿil and mafʿūl, the doer and the object from the iʿrāb masterclass, are not jargon. They are literally the root ف ع ل poured into these two moulds. The grammarians named the roles by demonstrating them.

Look what you already knew

ذِكْر — remembrance. ذَاكِر — one who remembers. مَذْكُور — mentioned. You have heard all three a thousand times without ever connecting them to ذَكَرَ. They were never separate words. They were one root, wearing three moulds.

Note too that كَانَ has no passive — nothing can be "be-ed." A verb that takes no object takes no passive, so the gap in that row is information, not an omission.

When the Verb
Comes First

Yesterday you learned a sentence with no verb in it at all. Today you meet its twin — the sentence that cannot wait, and puts the verb before it even tells you who did it.

Section 1Objectives

  1. Meet the verbal sentence (الجُمْلَةُ الفِعْلِيَّةُ) — and understand why Arabic loves to announce the action before the actor.
  2. Conjugate the past tense in the six forms you will actually hear, and recognise them instantly by their tails.
  3. Learn 15 verbs and nouns chosen from the highest-frequency layer of the Qur'an — words that also happen to run through every lecture ever given.
  4. Spot the moment when a plural subject takes a singular verb — a rule that looks like a mistake until you know it.
A word on today's word list

These fifteen were not chosen by taste. They sit inside the highest-frequency band of Qur'anic vocabulary — the few hundred words that make up the bulk of the text — and they are equally at home in a news bulletin. Learn a word here and you are paid twice.

Section 2Vocabulary — 15 Core Words

01فَعَلَ
he did, he acted
verb · past ف – ع – ل The pattern-word itself
مَاذَا فَعَلَ الرَّجُلُ؟

What did the man do?

This root is Arabic's measuring stick. Grammarians describe every verb pattern in the language using ف ع ل as the dummy — you will see faʿala, faʿʿala, afʿala used as templates for thousands of real verbs. Know it as a word and as a ruler.

02خَلَقَ
he created
verb · past خ – ل – ق Very high · Qur'anic
خَلَقَ اللهُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ.

Allah created the heavens and the earth.

Family: الْخَالِق the Creator · الْمَخْلُوق the created thing · خَلْق creation

03جَعَلَ
he made, he placed, he rendered (X into Y)
verb · past ج – ع – ل Top-tier Qur'anic
جَعَلَ اللهُ الْقُرْآنَ نُورًا.

Allah made the Qur'an a light.

Note it takes two objects — made the Qur'an (object 1) a light (object 2), and both wear a fatḥa. Contrast with خَلَقَ, which is creation from nothing; جَعَلَ is fashioning something into something.

04سَمِعَ
he heard, he listened
verb · past س – م – ع Your own project, in one word
سَمِعْتُ هٰذَا مِنَ الْأُسْتَاذِ.

I heard this from the teacher.

Family: السَّمِيع the All-Hearing · سَمْع hearing · اسْتَمَعَ he listened attentively — the verb for what you are training yourself to do.

05رَأَى
he saw; he was of the opinion
verb · past ر – أ – ي Top 20
رَأَيْنَا هٰذَا فِي الْكِتَابِ.

We saw this in the book.

In scholarly speech it means held the view: رَأَى الْإِمَامُ أَنَّ... — "the imam held that..." From it comes رَأْي, an opinion.

06عَلِمَ
he knew, he came to know
verb · past ع – ل – م Same root as yesterday's الْعِلْمُ
عَلِمَ الْعُلَمَاءُ هٰذِهِ الْمَسْأَلَةَ.

The scholars knew this issue.

Yesterday you learned the noun الْعِلْمُ. Today the verb from the same three letters. This is the compounding you were promised — one root quietly becoming a family.

07قَرَأَ
he read, he recited
verb · past ق – ر – أ The root of قُرْآن
قَرَأَ الشَّيْخُ الْآيَةَ ثُمَّ ذَكَرَ التَّفْسِيرَ.

The shaykh recited the verse, then mentioned the commentary.

The word Qur'an is built from this root — it means, quite literally, the Recitation. And the very first word revealed was its command form: اقْرَأْ — Read.

08كَتَبَ
he wrote; he prescribed, decreed
verb · past ك – ت – ب Top 30
كَتَبَ الطَّالِبُ الدَّرْسَ.

The student wrote the lesson.

Family: كِتَاب book · مَكْتَبَة library · كَاتِب writer · مَكْتُوب written, decreed

09أَخَذَ
he took, he seized
verb · past أ – خ – ذ Top 40
أَخَذَ الطَّالِبُ الْكِتَابَ.

The student took the book.

In lectures: نَأْخُذُ مِنْ هٰذَا — "we take from this [the following lesson]." A speaker's way of drawing a conclusion.

10دَخَلَ
he entered
verb · past د – خ – ل Top 60
دَخَلَ الرَّجُلُ الْمَسْجِدَ.

The man entered the mosque.

11خَرَجَ
he went out, he exited
verb · past خ – ر – ج The mirror of دَخَلَ
دَخَلَ ثُمَّ خَرَجَ.

He entered, then he went out.

Learn opposites in pairs. Your memory files them together and hands them back together.

12ثُمَّ
then, thereafter
connective particle Very high in narration
قَرَأَ الْآيَةَ ثُمَّ سَكَتَ.

He recited the verse, then fell silent.

Listening gold. ثُمَّ means then, after a gap. Its cousin فَـ means and then, immediately. When a speaker narrates a hadith, these two words are the joints of the story — hear them and you can feel the sequence even when you lose the nouns.

13الرَّسُولُ
the messenger
noun · masc. ر – س – ل Central to every lecture
قَالَ الرَّسُولُ ﷺ.

The Messenger ﷺ said.

Family: أَرْسَلَ he sent · رِسَالَة a message, a letter

14الْكِتَابُ
the book; the Book (scripture)
noun · masc. ك – ت – ب Top 30
أَخَذَ الْكِتَابَ وَقَرَأَهُ.

He took the book and read it.

Context decides everything: in a bookshop it is a book; on a shaykh's tongue, الْكِتَاب usually means the Book — the Qur'an.

15الْقَوْمُ
the people, the folk, the community
noun · collective ق – و – م Heavily Qur'anic
سَمِعَ الْقَوْمُ كَلَامَ الرَّسُولِ.

The people heard the Messenger's words.

Yesterday's النَّاس is people in general, humanity. الْقَوْم is a particular group — his people, that nation. The Qur'an says يَا قَوْمِ, "O my people," on the lips of prophet after prophet.

Section 3Ten Common Sentences

Yesterday's ten were verbless. Today's ten all move — and you will notice they nearly all begin with the verb.

01
خَلَقَ اللهُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ.

Allah created the heavens and the earth.

When you hear it: a Qur'anic formulation you will meet dozens of times. Note the order: verb, then doer. Arabic announces the act first.

02
قَالَ الرَّسُولُ ﷺ فِي الْحَدِيثِ...

The Messenger ﷺ said in the hadith...

When you hear it: a hadith is coming. Brace your attention — the next twenty seconds carry the point of the whole talk.

03
كَمَا سَمِعْتُمْ مِنْ قَبْلُ.

As you (all) have heard before.

When you hear it: the plural-you ending ـتُمْ tells you the speaker is addressing the room. He is calling back to something already said.

04
قَرَأَ الشَّيْخُ الْآيَةَ ثُمَّ ذَكَرَ التَّفْسِيرَ.

The shaykh recited the verse, then mentioned the commentary.

When you hear it: the standard shape of a tafsīr lesson. Verse first, explanation second, joined by ثُمَّ.

05
جَعَلَ اللهُ الْقُرْآنَ نُورًا وَهُدًى.

Allah made the Qur'an a light and a guidance.

When you hear it: a sermon's opening praise. هُدًى = guidance.

06
هَلْ سَمِعْتُمْ هٰذَا الْحَدِيثَ؟

Have you heard this hadith?

When you hear it: a rhetorical question — he does not want an answer, he wants your attention.

07
رَأَيْنَا هٰذَا فِي كِتَابِ اللهِ.

We have seen this in the Book of Allah.

When you hear it: note كِتَابِ اللهِ — the possession chain from the iʿrāb masterclass, live. Second noun in jarr, first noun stripped of الـ.

08
عَلِمَ الْعُلَمَاءُ أَنَّ هٰذَا حَقٌّ.

The scholars knew that this is true.

When you hear it: أَنَّ = "that" — the hinge that lets a speaker stack one statement inside another. It is everywhere.

09
دَخَلَ الرَّجُلُ الْمَسْجِدَ ثُمَّ خَرَجَ.

The man entered the mosque, then left.

When you hear it: the heartbeat of hadith narration — a chain of simple past verbs strung together with ثُمَّ and فَـ.

10
مَاذَا فَعَلَ الْقَوْمُ؟

What did the people do?

When you hear it: the storyteller's hook, thrown to the audience right before the answer.

Section 4Grammar — The Verbal Sentence

الجُمْلَةُ الفِعْلِيَّةُ

The default order is not the one you expect

English marches subject → verb → object: The student wrote the lesson. Classical Arabic prefers verb → subject → object:

كَتَبَ الطَّالِبُ الدَّرْسَ.

wrote — the student — the lesson.

Read it literally and it feels like a telegram: Wrote. The student. The lesson. But sit with it a moment and there is a logic. Arabic tells you what happened before it tells you who was involved. The action is the headline; the actors are the details. It is a language built for narration — which is exactly why the Qur'an and the hadith read the way they do.

The past tense: six tails to know

The verb stem stays put and a small tail is bolted on. That tail is the pronoun. There is no separate word for "I" or "we" — the ending is the subject.

Do not memorise the table. Memorise the tails: -at, -ū, -ta, -tum, -tu, -nā. Once those six sounds are lodged in your ear, you can hear any verb in Arabic — including ones you have never met — and know instantly who did it. Six sounds. Thousands of verbs. This is the best trade in the language.

The rule that looks like a mistake

Here is one that quietly confuses every learner. Look:

قَالَ الْعُلَمَاءُ.

The scholars said. — not qālū, but qāla. Singular verb, plural subject.

Why? Because when the verb comes first, it stays singular, no matter how many people follow it. The verb has not yet met its subject; it does not know how many are coming. Only when the subject is placed before the verb does the verb agree in number:

الْعُلَمَاءُ قَالُوا.

The scholars — they said. (Subject first, so now the verb wears its plural tail.)

That second version is a nominal sentence with a verbal predicate — yesterday's structure, wearing today's clothes. And the difference in flavour is real: the first states an event; the second puts the spotlight on the scholars, as if to say it was they who said it.

Section 5Reading Practice

دَرْسُ الشَّيْخِ

دَخَلَ الشَّيْخُ الْمَسْجِدَ، ثُمَّ جَلَسَ. سَمِعَ الْقَوْمُ صَوْتَهُ.

أَخَذَ الْكِتَابَ وَقَرَأَ آيَةً مِنْ كِتَابِ اللهِ. ثُمَّ قَالَ: خَلَقَ اللهُ النَّاسَ، وَجَعَلَ لَهُمُ الْعِلْمَ.

كَتَبَ الطَّالِبُ كُلَّ شَيْءٍ. عَلِمَ أَنَّ هٰذَا أَمْرٌ مُهِمٌّ.

ثُمَّ خَرَجَ الشَّيْخُ، وَخَرَجَ الْقَوْمُ مَعَهُ.

The Shaykh's Lesson

The shaykh entered the mosque, then sat down. The people heard his voice.

He took the book and recited a verse from the Book of Allah. Then he said: Allah created people, and made knowledge for them.

The student wrote everything down. He knew that this was an important matter.

Then the shaykh went out, and the people went out with him.

New in context: جَلَسَ he sat · صَوْت voice · آيَة verse · لَهُمْ for them · مَعَ with

Look what you just read

Count the verbs at the start of their clauses: دَخَلَ · سَمِعَ · أَخَذَ · قَرَأَ · قَالَ · خَلَقَ · كَتَبَ · خَرَجَ. Eight verbs, eight sentences opening on the action. That drumbeat — verb, verb, verb, stitched with ثُمَّ — is the native rhythm of Arabic narrative prose. You have just read a paragraph that is structurally identical to a hadith.

Section 6Listening Practice

Scenario: a shaykh opens a lesson on a hadith. Read it aloud twice — once slowly, once at pace.

الشَّيْخُ:

الْحَمْدُ لِلهِ. الْيَوْمَ نَقْرَأُ حَدِيثًا مُهِمًّا.

كَمَا سَمِعْتُمْ مِنْ قَبْلُ، قَالَ الرَّسُولُ ﷺ كَلِمَةً قَصِيرَةً، وَلٰكِنَّ فِيهَا عِلْمًا كَثِيرًا.

دَخَلَ رَجُلٌ الْمَسْجِدَ، ثُمَّ سَأَلَ الرَّسُولَ ﷺ عَنِ الْإِسْلَامِ.

فَقَالَ لَهُ الرَّسُولُ ﷺ... وَهٰذَا هُوَ الْحَدِيثُ.

طَالِبٌ:

يَا شَيْخُ، مَنْ رَوَىٰ هٰذَا الْحَدِيثَ؟

الشَّيْخُ:

سُؤَالٌ جَيِّدٌ. رَوَاهُ الْبُخَارِيُّ.

Shaykh: All praise is for Allah. Today we are reading an important hadith. As you have heard before, the Messenger ﷺ said a short word — but within it is a great deal of knowledge. A man entered the mosque, then asked the Messenger ﷺ about Islam. So the Messenger ﷺ said to him... and this is the hadith.

Student: Shaykh, who narrated this hadith?

Shaykh: A good question. Al-Bukhārī narrated it.

Say it yourself

Speaking begins in small pieces, not in speeches. Say these three aloud, right now, without looking at the English:

سَمِعْتُ هٰذَا مِنْ قَبْلُ.

عِنْدِي سُؤَالٌ، يَا شَيْخُ.

قَرَأْتُ الْكِتَابَ.

Three sentences you could actually use tomorrow. That is what fluency is made of — not grammar you can recite, but sentences your mouth already knows.

Section 8Derivation Table

The grids above answered one question: who did it? This table answers a different one, and a bigger one — what else can this root become?

Arabic does not really have words. It has roots, and a set of moulds you pour them into. Pour ك ت ب into the doer-mould and you get كَاتِب, a writer. Pour it into the done-to mould and you get مَكْتُوب, something written. The moulds never change. Learn them once and every root in the language starts handing you free words.

The two moulds that matter most

The doer — فَاعِل: fāʿil. Take the root, pour it in: خَالِق (creator), عَالِم (knower, scholar), سَامِع (hearer), قَارِئ (reciter).

The done-to — مَفْعُول: mafʿūl. Same root, other mould: مَخْلُوق (a created thing), مَعْلُوم (known), مَسْمُوع (heard), مَكْتُوب (written, decreed).

And notice — the words fāʿil and mafʿūl that you met in the iʿrāb masterclass, the doer and the object? They are not jargon. They are literally ف ع ل poured into these same two moulds. The grammarians named the roles by demonstrating them.

خَالِق · سَامِع · عَالِم · قَارِئ — the Creator, the Hearer, the Knower, the Reciter. Three of those are Names of Allah, and you did not learn them as vocabulary. You generated them from verbs you already knew, using a single mould.

This is the compounding that makes Arabic worth the climb. Somewhere around your two-thousandth word, you will stop meeting new words and start meeting old roots in new clothes — and your rate of learning will quietly double.

One thing to actually notice

Look at the passive: كَتَبَ (he wrote) becomes كُتِبَ (it was written). No new word — only the vowels changed. kataba → kutiba. Same three letters, same consonant skeleton.

This is why iʿrāb and vowelling are not decoration. In a lecture, katab and kutib are one syllable apart, and they are the difference between a man writing and a decree being written. Your ear must learn to hear u–i as the signature of the passive. When you hear it, ask: who did this, and why is the speaker not telling me?

The two frames, side by side

Nominal — الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ — no verb. Describes a state. Both halves in rafʿ.

Verbal — قَرَأَ الشَّيْخُ الْآيَةَ — verb first. Describes an event. Doer in rafʿ, target in naṣb.

Every Arabic sentence you will ever hear is one of these two. Two frames. That is the entire architecture.

Iʿrāb
Why the last letter keeps changing

You did not fail this topic. You were taught it in a language you were still learning, using words like fāʿil and manṣūb that were never translated. Today, nothing goes untranslated.

Section 1The One Idea

Forget everything for sixty seconds and look at English:

SentenceWord usedWhy
He saw the teacher.hehe is doing
The teacher saw him.himhe is receiving
The book is his.hishe is owning

Same person. Three different shapes: he / him / his. English changes the word to show the job.

Arabic does exactly this — but instead of changing the whole word, it changes only the last vowel. And it does it to every noun in the language, not just to pronouns.

The definition, in one line

Iʿrāb (إِعْرَاب) is the system of endings that tells you what job a word is doing in the sentence. The ending is a job title, pronounced out loud.

That is the whole topic. Everything below is just detail.

Section 2The Three Jobs, The Three Endings

Arabic nouns have exactly three states. Not thirty. Three.

The doubled version

Each ending has a plain form and a doubled form. The doubled one — tanwīn — simply means "a / an":

الْكِتَابُ = the book  ·  كِتَابٌ = a book

So the ending carries two pieces of information at once: the job and the definiteness. One syllable, two facts. This is why Arabic can be so compact.

One spelling quirk to know now

When an indefinite noun takes naṣb, Arabic writes an extra alif at the end: كِتَابًا — kitāban. That silent alif is nothing more than a chair for the fatḥatayn to sit on. It is not a letter you pronounce.

Section 3One Sentence, All Three

Here is the sentence that makes the whole system click. Read the endings aloud.

كَتَبَ الطَّالِبُ الدَّرْسَ فِي الْكِتَابِ.

The student wrote the lesson in the book.

WordEndingStateBecause...
الطَّالِبُ-u  ـُrafʿHe is the doer. He did the writing.
الدَّرْسَ-a  ـَnaṣbIt is the target. The writing landed on it.
الْكِتَابِ-i  ـِjarrIt follows the preposition فِي.

Three words, three different final vowels, three different jobs. Nothing else in the sentence tells you who wrote what. The vowels are doing all the work.

Section 4The Proof: Why Arabic Needs This

Now watch what iʿrāb buys you. Take a sentence:

ضَرَبَ زَيْدٌ عَمْرًا.

Zayd hit ʿAmr.

Now scramble the word order — put ʿAmr first:

ضَرَبَ عَمْرًا زَيْدٌ.

Zayd hit ʿAmr. Still. The meaning has not moved an inch.

Sit with this

In English, "the dog bit the man" and "the man bit the dog" are different events — because English encodes meaning in word order. Arabic encodes it in endings. Zayd carries -un (doer) wherever he stands; ʿAmr carries -an (target) wherever he stands.

This is why Arabic poetry, Qur'anic verses and classical prose can rearrange words so freely and still be perfectly clear. The endings are the glue.

And it is why the endings matter to you, the listener. In a formal lecture the speaker pronounces them. Every -u you catch is the speaker telling you: this is my subject, hold onto it. Every -i says: this one is just hanging off a preposition, don't over-weight it. You are being handed a live grammatical commentary, for free, in real time.

Tie-back to Chapter 1

Look again: الْعِلْمُ نُورٌ. Both words end in a ḍamma sound. That matching pair of -u endings is the audible fingerprint of a nominal sentence. Hear two -us in a row with no verb — you are inside a "X is Y" sentence.

Think of naṣb as Arabic's default bin for everything extra. If a word is not the doer and not chained to a preposition, it is probably wearing a fatḥa.

The iḍāfa, in one breath

كِتَابُ الطَّالِبِ = "the book of the student." The first noun takes whatever ending its job in the sentence requires. The second noun is always jarr. Always. No exceptions. And the first noun never takes الـ, because the second one already makes it definite.

You already met one: عِنْدَ الْعُلَمَاءِ — "in the view of the scholars." Hear that final -i? That is an iḍāfa announcing itself.

Section 6The Two Sentence-Flippers

Two families of words exist for one purpose: to change the endings of the sentence that follows them. Once you know them, half of "why is that ending like that?" is answered.

① كَانَ and its sisters — the khabar gets knocked down to naṣb

الْعِلْمُ نَافِعٌ.

Knowledge is beneficial. (both rafʿ: -u ... -un)

كَانَ الْعِلْمُ نَافِعًا.

Knowledge was beneficial. (subject stays rafʿ; the predicate flips to -an)

Sisters of كان that behave identically: لَيْسَ (is not), أَصْبَحَ (became), مَا زَالَ (still is), صَارَ (became).

This is why Chapter 1's example was لَيْسَ الْأَمْرُ سَهْلًا — sahlan, not sahlun. Now you know exactly who did that to it.

② إِنَّ and its sisters — the mirror image

إِنَّ الْعِلْمَ نُورٌ.

Indeed, knowledge is light. (subject drops to -a; predicate stays -un)

Exactly the reverse of كان. Sisters: أَنَّ (that), لٰكِنَّ (but), لَعَلَّ (perhaps), كَأَنَّ (as if).

Why this pays off immediately

Islamic lectures are saturated with إِنَّ. Every time you hear إِنَّ اللهَ — inna Allāha — with that fatḥa, that is not random. It is إِنَّ forcing the noun into naṣb. You have heard this a thousand times. Now you know why.

Section 7The Good News: Many Words Never Change

Here is the mercy in the system. A large slice of the most common Arabic words are mabnī (مَبْنِيّ) — "built solid." They are frozen. They never take iʿrāb endings at all.

هٰذَاهٰذِهِالَّذِيهُوَهِيَ أَنَانَحْنُمَنْمَاكَيْفَ

Demonstratives, relative pronouns, personal pronouns, question words — all frozen. Every past-tense verb is frozen too (قَالَ is always qāla).

So when your teacher parsed هٰذَا and said مَبْنِيٌّ عَلَى السُّكُونِ فِي مَحَلِّ رَفْعٍ — "frozen, but occupying the position of rafʿ" — he meant: this word is doing the doer's job, but it refuses to wear the doer's badge. You still know its job. You just cannot hear it on the ending.

Section 9Verbs Have Iʿrāb Too

Only the present-tense verb changes its ending, and it has three states. This is the entire system:

StateEndingTriggerExample
rafʿ — default-u  ـُnothing in front of itيَكْتُبُ — he writes
naṣb-a  ـَأَنْ (to), لَنْ (will not), كَيْ (in order to)لَنْ يَكْتُبَ — he will not write
jazmsukūn  ـْلَمْ (did not), لَا (don't!), إِنْ (if)لَمْ يَكْتُبْ — he did not write

Jazm (جَزْم) is a fourth state that exists only for verbs — nouns never take it. It means "cut short," which is exactly what the sukūn does to the sound.

Instant payoff

لَمْ takes a present-tense verb but produces a past meaning. لَمْ يَقُلْ = "he did not say." Beginners hear the present-tense shape and mistranslate it as "he does not say." The little لَمْ — and the clipped ending — are the tell.

Section 10Parse a Sentence Like a Scholar

Doing iʿrāb on a sentence means naming each word's job and ending. Here is the ritual, done once, slowly.

ذَكَرَ الْأُسْتَاذُ الْحَقَّ فِي دَرْسِ الْيَوْمِ.

The teacher mentioned the truth in today's lesson.

Notice دَرْسِ has no الـ and no tanwīn. That is the signature of the first half of an iḍāfa — it borrows its definiteness from الْيَوْمِ.

Your three-question decoder

For any noun you meet, ask in this order:

1. Is it after a preposition, or possessed by the word before it? → jarr, kasra.
2. Is it the one doing the action, or the topic of a verbless sentence? → rafʿ, ḍamma.
3. Neither? → almost certainly naṣb, fatḥa.

Three questions. That is the whole of iʿrāb for a reader.

Section 11Exercises

A. Name the state and say why

For each underlined word, give the state (rafʿ / naṣb / jarr) and the reason.

  1. قَالَ الرَّجُلُ الْحَقَّ. → الرَّجُلُ
  2. قَالَ الرَّجُلُ الْحَقَّ. → الْحَقَّ
  3. تَكَلَّمْنَا عَنِ الْعِلْمِ. → الْعِلْمِ
  4. إِنَّ الْعِلْمَ نُورٌ. → الْعِلْمَ
  5. كَانَ الْأَمْرُ مُهِمًّا. → مُهِمًّا
  6. كِتَابُ الطَّالِبِ جَدِيدٌ. → الطَّالِبِ

B. Supply the missing ending

  1. ذَكَرَ الْأُسْتَاذ__ الدَّرْس__ .   (The teacher mentioned the lesson.)
  2. إِنَّ النَّاس__ كَثِيرُونَ.   (Indeed, the people are many.)
  3. لَيْسَ الْأَمْرُ سَهْل__ .   (The matter is not easy.)
  4. تَكَلَّمَ عَنْ شَيْء__ مُهِمّ__ .   (He spoke about an important thing.)

C. Same words, different endings — different meaning?

Translate both. Then say what changed.

  1. رَأَى الْمُسْلِمُونَ الْأُسْتَاذَ.
  2. رَأَى الْأُسْتَاذُ الْمُسْلِمِينَ.

D. Verb states

Which state is the verb in, and what triggered it?

  1. لَنْ يَذْكُرَ هٰذَا.
  2. لَمْ يَذْكُرْ هٰذَا.
  3. يَذْكُرُ هٰذَا كَثِيرًا.
Answer key

A. 1. rafʿ — it is the fāʿil, the doer of "said." · 2. naṣb — it is the object, what was said. · 3. jarr — it follows the preposition عَنْ. · 4. naṣb — إِنَّ forces its noun into naṣb. · 5. naṣb — كَانَ forces its predicate into naṣb. · 6. jarr — second noun of an iḍāfa.

B. 1. الْأُسْتَاذُ الدَّرْسَ · 2. النَّاسَ · 3. سَهْلًا · 4. شَيْءٍ مُهِمٍّ (the adjective follows its noun into jarr)

C. 1. "The Muslims saw the teacher" — الْمُسْلِمُونَ ends in -ūna, so they are the doers. 2. "The teacher saw the Muslims" — now الْمُسْلِمِينَ ends in -īna, so they are the target. Only the endings changed the meaning.

D. 1. naṣb, triggered by لَنْ — "he will not mention this." · 2. jazm, triggered by لَمْ — "he did not mention this" (past meaning, present-tense shape). · 3. rafʿ, nothing in front of it — "he mentions this often."

Section 12How to Actually Absorb This

Iʿrāb is not learned by memorising the table. It is learned by predicting.

  1. Read aloud with endings. Take any fully-vowelled sentence in this book and pronounce every final vowel, even though native speakers often drop them in casual speech. Your mouth teaches your ear.
  2. Play "guess the ending." Before you reach the end of a word in a text, predict its vowel from its job. Check. You will be wrong often at first and rarely within a month.
  3. Hunt for إِنَّ in lectures. Every time you hear it, listen for the fatḥa on the next noun. This single habit will make the system audible to you.
A last word

Your first teacher was not wrong to teach iʿrāb — he was teaching you the load-bearing wall of the entire language. He simply built it in Arabic, on a foundation you did not have yet. You have the foundation now. Three states, three vowels, three questions. That is all it ever was.
